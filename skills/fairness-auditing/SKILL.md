# Fairness Auditing

**Core principle**: Fairness is not a single property — it is a family of competing definitions, most of which are mathematically incompatible. An effective audit names which definition applies, measures whether it holds, and identifies where in the pipeline bias enters so it can be addressed at the source rather than papered over at the output.

---

## When to Use This Skill

- A system, algorithm, or policy produces different outcome rates across demographic or protected groups
- Someone asks whether an automated decision process is biased or discriminatory
- A scoring, ranking, or filtering mechanism is being designed or reviewed
- Outcome data reveals unexplained disparities
- Regulatory or compliance review requires fairness documentation
- An ethical-reasoning analysis flags distributional concerns that need quantitative follow-up

---

## Core Methodology

### Step 1: Scope the Audit

Identify the system under review and its decision boundary. Define:
- **Decision**: What outcome does the system produce? (accept/reject, score, rank, allocate)
- **Affected population**: Who is subject to the decision?
- **Protected attributes**: Which group memberships are relevant? (race, gender, age, disability, income, geography — context-dependent)
- **Ground truth**: Is there a defensible definition of "correct" outcome, or is the outcome inherently value-laden?

Without a clear scope, audits drift into vague concerns. Pin down exactly what decision, for whom, across which groups.

### Step 2: Select Fairness Definitions

Choose which fairness criteria apply. These are often mutually exclusive — selecting one means accepting trade-offs on others. The five major definitions:

- **Demographic parity**: Outcome rates are equal across groups. Appropriate when there is no legitimate reason for differential rates.
- **Equal opportunity**: True positive rates are equal across groups. Appropriate when the goal is equal access to a beneficial outcome for those who qualify.
- **Predictive parity**: Precision is equal across groups. Appropriate when the system's predictions must be equally reliable for all groups.
- **Individual fairness**: Similar individuals receive similar outcomes regardless of group membership. Appropriate when a meaningful similarity metric exists.
- **Counterfactual fairness**: The outcome would not change if the individual's protected attribute were different, all else equal. Appropriate for causal analysis of specific decisions.

State which definitions you are applying and why. Flag when chosen definitions conflict with each other — this is a value judgment, not a technical one.

### Step 3: Audit the Data

Examine the inputs the system uses:

- **Historical bias**: Does the training or reference data reflect past discrimination? (e.g., hiring data from a period of exclusion)
- **Label bias**: Were the ground-truth labels generated by a biased process? (e.g., arrest records as proxy for crime rates)
- **Representation gaps**: Are some groups underrepresented, leading to worse model performance for those groups?
- **Proxy variables**: Do ostensibly neutral features (zip code, name, school) correlate strongly with protected attributes and effectively leak group membership into the decision?

For each finding, note whether the bias is correctable at the data level or is baked into the problem definition.

### Step 4: Measure Outcome Disparities

Quantify actual outcome rates across groups:

- Compute acceptance/rejection rates, score distributions, or allocation shares per group
- Calculate disparity ratios (e.g., selection rate of group A / selection rate of group B)
- Apply the four-fifths rule where applicable: a ratio below 0.8 signals adverse impact under US employment law
- Test for statistical significance — small samples can produce large-looking disparities by chance
- Check calibration: does the same score mean the same thing across groups?

Report raw numbers alongside ratios. Disparity ratios without base rates are misleading.

### Step 5: Conduct Intersectionality Analysis

Fairness can hold for each protected attribute independently but fail at intersections:

- Cross-tabulate outcomes by combinations of protected attributes (e.g., race x gender, age x disability)
- Check whether subgroups at intersections experience compounded disadvantage
- Verify that sample sizes at intersections support meaningful conclusions — flag where they do not

Do not assume that group-level fairness implies subgroup-level fairness.

### Step 6: Recommend Mitigations

For each identified disparity, propose interventions at the appropriate stage:

- **Pre-processing**: Debias or rebalance training data, remove or transform proxy variables, augment underrepresented groups
- **In-processing**: Add fairness constraints to the optimization objective, use adversarial debiasing, apply regularization that penalizes group disparity
- **Post-processing**: Adjust decision thresholds per group to equalize target metrics, apply reject-option classification

For each mitigation, state the trade-off: what fairness metric improves, what accuracy or other metric may degrade, and whether the trade-off is acceptable given the stakes.

## Common Traps

- **Fairness gerrymandering** — choosing whichever fairness definition makes the system look best. State your definition up front and justify it.
- **Proxy blindness** — removing the protected attribute but leaving correlated features that reconstruct it. Audit proxies, not just explicit attributes.
- **Aggregate fairness, subgroup harm** — overall parity can mask severe disparities at intersections. Always check intersectionality.
- **Snapshot auditing** — measuring fairness once and assuming it holds. Systems drift; feedback loops amplify small biases over time. Build ongoing monitoring.
- **False precision** — reporting disparity ratios to three decimal places on small samples. Report confidence intervals and flag inadequate sample sizes.

## Thinking Triggers

- *"Which fairness definition are we implicitly using, and who chose it?"*
- *"If we removed the protected attribute entirely, would the outcomes actually change?"*
- *"What does 'fair' mean to the people most affected by this system?"*
- *"Are we optimizing for the appearance of fairness or for actual equitable outcomes?"*
- *"What feedback loops could amplify today's small disparity into tomorrow's large one?"*
